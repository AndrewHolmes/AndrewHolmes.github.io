Textile Mill Redesign

During Summer 2017 I worked with a professor of anthropology at Olin and a team of three other students to do a research and design project with a 150 year old textile mill. We studied the effects of automation, deindustrialization, and the Made in America movement on the industry by conducting fieldwork on the production floor and working different jobs throughout the manufacturing process. We redesigned a part of the production floor in a four week project, using our experience on the floor and conversations with management and employees to better understand inefficiencies in the production line. Taking the time to work different jobs throughout the factory allowed us to better understand the people, machines, processes, and complex systems that define the mill before attempting to make major changes. Over the course of the project I developed skills in manufacturing engineering, consulting work with company managers, co-design with employees, rapid prototyping, and anthropological fieldwork.

While conducting fieldwork I worked in the carding section to collect large bags of "pile" that would eventually become knit together into full pieces of fabric.

We ultimately decided to redesign the chute used to transfer product (large, 50-80 pound bundles of fabric) from upstairs to downstairs. The chute feeds into a machine at the bottom and must be stocked with product from a storage area upstairs so that employees downstairs can do their job. Originally, transferring product required that the employee in their quality assurance department behind the chute to stop what they were doing, bend into the box surrounding the chute to determine if the chute must be loaded, obtain the fabric bundles from the storage area, and flip it onto the floor and into the chute. As we worked in this department, we found the task to be strenuous, distracting from the primary task in quality assurance, and time consuming. We discussed each aspect of the process with employees on the floor and higher-ups in the company to reach the conclusion that this was an area we could improve upon.

The chute as it appeared before the project started.

Our main goal in redesigning the chute was to create a feeding system that would require less effort from employees without costing more time than manually feeding. We also addressed other smaller issues that we found, such as fabric getting caught as it slides, a lack of visibility from the work station, product flipping upsidedown during feeding, and heat and noise pollution from downstairs. Within the first week of the project we removed the old box that covered the chute, relined the walls of with smooth plastic that fabric would not get caught on, and built an inclined ramp that would catch the fabric and help to prevent it from flipping over. By removing the box and installing a mirror, employees could determine if they had to feed the chute without leaving their workstation.

We worked with maintenance to remove the large box from the ground.

At this point we had the freedom to experiment with different methods of feeding the chute, with four different angles from which we could approach the gaping hole in the floor. We tested over 30 different ideas, creating quick mock-up prototypes of different mechanisms and methods. We used electric palet jacks to simulate a tilting mechanism that could slide fabric from the floor and into the chute, created a pushing stick that employees could use to quickly shove fabric without having to bend over, placed thin plastic sheets between the stacked bundles of fabric to make it easier for them to slide into the chute, and created a block and tackle pulley system suspended from the ceiling for lifting fabric and lowering it into the chute. While all of these ideas satisfied at least one critical metric (time to use, usability, cost, time to build, required effort), none of them were adequate enough to move past the prototyping stage. Running out of time, the team reached an impass.

My three teammates work with an employee during construction to test different methods of feeding the chute.

We learned through this process that for the employees on the floor, even the most high-tech innovations are not going to be satisfactory if they come at the cost of time. Even though management valued safety above anything else, our core user was ultimately the employee, and their needs and values were not the same as those in the office. Additionally, we found that there are too many inconsistencies in how their fabrics are folded and prepared that a universal system was too challenging to apply, however, we were able to address a major inefficiency created by their heaviest fabrics. These 200 pound bundles of fabric could not fit in the chute and took 10 minutes to cart to the other side of the mill, down a freight elevator, and back to the bottom of the chute. Now without the box on the chute, we were able to load the large bundles by tilting them into the chute with a pallet jack, reducing a 10 minute/bundle process to about 2 minutes.

Throughout the project we visited several other factories and companies involved in the textile industry, including Joseph Abboud and New Balance. Seeing the production floors of large, modern companies and understanding how companies organize their factory floor and strike a balance between humans and machines helped to better frame the inefficiencies we were observing in the mill and how we could address them.
